💬 Public Comments

Sausalito City Council Meeting – April 21, 2026: Public Comment Analysis

Agenda Item 5A: Capital Improvement Program (CIP) Projects for FY 2026/27

CIP Priorities and Gaps

  • Support for specific projects: 4 comments
  • Joris van Mens raised questions about the Spring Street Traffic Lights ($800k) and Princess Street Improvements, and requested funding for the Cycling Master Plan.
  • Kieran Culligan offered detailed recommendations: update the Bicycle Master Plan, maintain funding for Princess Street improvements, repair Langendorf Steps, plan for Bridgeway (Gate 6 to Harbor Drive) improvements, adopt Complete Streets lane widths for resurfacing, and implement AB 413 daylighting citywide.
  • Carolyn Revelle (Sausalito Beautiful) supported the street tree replacement allocation ($60,000), the City Parks Master Plan, and Cloudview Park access improvements, and offered ongoing partnership.

  • Illina Frankiv echoed many of Culligan's points: update the Bicycle Master Plan, keep the Napa roundabout concept on schedule, maintain Princess Street funding, advance Bridgeway planning (Gate 6 to Harbor Drive), and apply Complete Streets lane widths in all resurfacing.

  • Key themes: Need for bicycle and pedestrian infrastructure investment; concern that the CIP lacks new projects for non-vehicle users; requests to align with Complete Streets Policy; requests for transparency and public information on project details; support for tree replacement and parks planning.

Agenda Item 5B: Implementing Daylighting (AB 413) on Caledonia Street

  • Support for immediate daylighting compliance: ~30 comments — Nearly every commenter supported implementing AB 413 daylighting on Caledonia Avenue without further delay. Arguments included: the state deadline passed 14+ months ago; a pedestrian was injured at Caledonia and Turney in 2025; daylighting improves sightlines and saves lives; further delay exposes the City to legal liability; "Optional Task A" (reducing safety buffers) should be rejected; paint-only compliance should happen immediately even if broader planning takes longer.

  • Opposition to the current staff recommendation (narrow traffic engineering study): ~25 comments — Commenters did not oppose daylighting itself, but strongly opposed limiting the scope to a traffic engineering study. They urged a comprehensive urban design / landscape architecture-led approach instead. Arguments included: the loss of ~21 parking spaces will hurt Caledonia businesses; surrounding streets have ample underutilized road space that could yield 100+ new parking spaces with creative redesign; the Bridgeway safety project, while effective, was aesthetically poor — Caledonia should not repeat that mistake; landscape architects should lead (not traffic engineers); the Caledonia Merchants Association and community stakeholders should be included in the process.

  • Key themes:

  • Safety urgency: Paint the curbs red now; do not delay legally mandated safety measures for a parking study.
  • Design-forward vision: Hire a landscape architect to lead a holistic streetscape redesign integrating safety, parking, beautification, greening, pedestrian flow, and economic vitality.
  • Parking concerns: Loss of ~21 spaces is a serious threat to local businesses; redesigning surrounding side streets could offset and even increase total parking.
  • Avoid Bridgeway mistakes: Multiple commenters cited the recent Bridgeway pedestrian improvements as functional but ugly, and urged a more aesthetically sensitive approach for Caledonia.
  • Community engagement: Business owners (Sushi Ran, Driver's Market, Tivoli Decor, Sausalito Picture Framing, Studio 333, The Marin Merchant, J. Roland Salon) and the Caledonia Merchants Association want to be involved in shaping solutions.
  • Liability warning: Kieran Culligan explicitly warned that delaying paint-only compliance while a study proceeds creates indefensible legal liability if another pedestrian is struck.
  • Downtown Sausalito Association (Kass Green) noted they have already retained the firm SWA to develop streetscape alternatives for downtown and offered to partner with the City.

Agenda Item 5C: Resident Parking Benefit Program

  • Support for Option 1 (Maintain Existing Program): ~17 comments — The majority of commenters on this item strongly supported continuing the free 3-hour resident parking benefit with the new ParkMobile/PIN-code cardless system. Arguments included: the program has been a community asset for 50 years; it encourages residents (especially seniors) to visit downtown and support local businesses; modernizing to ParkMobile reduces administrative costs; the program fosters community connection; eliminating or charging for parking would disproportionately affect seniors on fixed incomes.

  • Notable supporters: Tom Anderson & Dana Christensen, Mark Rushford, Michelle Mokalla, Molly Haun, Lou Masiero, Sam Ruben, Jacques Ullman, Jann Johnson, Jenny Court, Kara Halvorsen, Kathy White, Beth Swierk, David Kornmeyer, Megan Gathers, Naomi Smith, Laura Rafaty, Victoria Anderson, Carroll Covey (with concerns about ease of use for seniors), and the Downtown Sausalito Association (Carolyn Revelle & Amy Svendberg), which also offered to host a community information session on ParkMobile.

  • Support for Option 4 (Reduced Rate Hybrid Program): 3 comments — Kieran Culligan, Joris van Mens, and Illina Frankiv supported a reduced-rate option rather than fully free parking. Arguments included: subsidized free parking creates artificial shortages and increases circling traffic; a small charge (~$0.45/3 hours) preserves a local benefit while generating $40K–$70K in annual city revenue; encourages walking/biking for short trips; improves parking availability; Illina Frankiv additionally urged expanding bike parking near businesses and prioritizing accessibility for those with mobility limitations.

  • Key themes:

  • Strong majority favor keeping the program free (Option 1) with modernized technology.
  • Seniors and fixed-income residents are particularly reliant on this benefit.
  • Concerns from at least one senior (Carroll Covey) about difficulty using new pay station technology.
  • A minority favors a modest charge (Option 4) to improve turnover, generate revenue, and encourage alternative transportation.
  • The Downtown Sausalito Association supports Option 1 and offered to help educate residents on the new system.

Non-Agenda-Item Public Comment

Constituent Correspondence and ADU Application Process — Jake Byer

  • 1 comment — Jake Byer submitted a detailed letter (originally sent March 16, 2026, and ignored) requesting:
  • Implementation of a ticket tracking system for constituent emails and inquiries, citing over 40 unanswered emails to City staff over six months regarding denied ADU and JADU applications.
  • Quarterly public reporting on staff response rates and unanswered inquiries.
  • Compliance with the Permit Streamlining Act (30-day completeness determination, 60-day ADU approval timeline) and the Housing Accountability Act.
  • Key themes: Systemic failure of governmental accountability; obstruction through silence on housing applications; lack of responsiveness from the Community Development Department and City Attorney; the commenter's prior public comment was itself ignored and excluded from the record.

Traffic Safety at Tomales & Wateree (MLK Campus) — Austin Arensberg

  • 1 comment — Austin Arensberg requested urgent action on a dangerous intersection near the new MLK campus, where students cross without stop signs. He asked the Council to:
  • Direct a formal traffic study for stop sign installation at Tomales and Wateree.
  • Immediately paint 20-foot red zones at the intersection per AB 413.
  • Key themes: Child pedestrian safety; near-misses at an uncontrolled intersection; a prior safety study has stalled; urgency before a serious accident occurs.
